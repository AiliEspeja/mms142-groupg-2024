What is Grab?

Grab is a technology company based in Southeast Asia, best known for its ride-hailing services. It was founded in 2012 in Malaysia and has since expanded its operations across various countries in the region, including Singapore, Indonesia, Thailand, Vietnam, and the Philippines. In addition to ride-hailing, Grab has diversified its offerings to include food delivery services through GrabFood, digital payments and financial services through GrabPay, and logistics services through GrabExpress. The app provides a platform for users to book rides, order food, and make payments, making it a multifaceted super app popular in Southeast Asia.


How were things before Grab?

Before Grab, most people used public transportation. In cities, these included jeepneys, buses, trains, and tricycles. The Public Utility Vehicle Modernization Program (PUVMP) in the Philippines aims to improve the safety, convenience, and efficiency of public transportation, particularly addressing the shortcomings of traditional jeepneys, which are often viewed as uncomfortable and unsafe. A mixed-method study assessed the impact of jeepney design on passenger comfort and safety, using heart rate monitoring to gauge experiences. Findings revealed that cramped interiors, overcrowding, and exposure to the environment led to higher and more erratic heart rates in traditional jeepney passengers compared to those in modernized versions. The study found that modern jeepneys (MPUJs), designed with features like air conditioning and improved ventilation, significantly reduced discomfort and stress, aligning with theoretical expectations regarding heart rate and comfort. Participants showed a strong preference for MPUJs after experiencing their benefits, indicating a positive shift in attitudes toward public transport improvements. However, concerns about potential job losses from phasing out traditional jeepneys remain prevalent. The research underscores the importance of design in enhancing passenger experiences and highlights areas for continued improvement in the Philippine public transportation system.




How is Grab a Disruptive Technology?

Before Grab: The Struggle of Commuting in the Philippines

The chaos of transportation before Grab existed was unimaginable. Transport terminals were all packed with options: Buses, jeepneys, tricycles through neighborhoods. It wasn’t simple! Coming from the south and heading to the north? You would have to stop at lots of terminals, switch rides, and repeat the cycle. Traveling from province to city because of work? It would mean long and sweaty lines for jeepneys and buses. Even if you were already late—you just had to deal with it. That is the reality that Filipinos go through.


The Game-Changer for Commuters

Grab was finally introduced to us. A reliable, convenient, and comfortable alternative! With a few taps, you could already grab a ride. They can pick you up and drop you off exactly where you need to go—no more worrying about sweaty and long lines at the terminals.




Lifestyle Impacts

Convenience in Commuting and Shopping Habits

Grab has transformed urban living, making commutes, traveling, and shopping hassle-free! Gone are the days of battling with other people lining up for public transportation. With services like GrabFood and GrabMart, users can enjoy grocery and shopping without having to leave their homes. It was such a game-changer for those with hectic schedules.


Time-Saving Benefits and Lifestyle Adjustments

Grab’s efficiency gives the users a chance to reclaim their valuable time—the time that can be spent on hobbies, work, or other activities. This shift is a perfect match for career-driven, tech-savvy urban residents! With easier access for delivery services and ride-hailing, the reliance on private vehicles has decreased.




Transportation and Delivery Transformation

Evolution of Delivery Services!

The food and package delivery in the Philippines used to be limited to urban areas and more rural areas, before the pandemic. Services like GrabFood and Foodpanda led the market. However, underdeveloped infrastructure left some areas underserved, and was limiting consumer choice. The COVID-19 hit—and the boom… everything has changed. Due to lockdowns and social distancing, the demand for delivery services increased. From new service categories to expanded coverage, the industry made it possible to meet the needs of a world staying home. Today, delivery services aren’t just convenient—they are now essentials! The boom in online shopping has raised the bar, consumers expecting quicker, smarter, and greener solutions. Companies are now embracing eco-friendly practices, and cutting-edge tech are now leading the charge in a competitive market.




Social and Urban Impacts

Enhanced Urban Mobility and Traffic Congestion

Grab has improved urban mobility in Southeast Asia by offering accessible ride-hailing and delivery services that fill public transportation gaps. Their use of GPS technology and dynamic pricing helps optimize routes and reduce commute times. However, this rapid growth has led to increased traffic congestion, as many users prefer ride-hailing over public transport, especially in cities with inefficient mass transit systems. To address these challenges, Grab has partnered with the UNDP to reduce pollution and congestion through initiatives promoting clean vehicles, better traffic management, and improved road safety. In cities like Phnom Penh, Grab's network of over 2.4 million drivers provides valuable data for optimizing routes and enhancing public transport alternatives (Grab Cambodia, 2018).


Accessibility Challenges for Rural Areas and Low-Income Groups

Rural and low-income communities continue to face significant barriers to accessing Grab’s services, including limited digital literacy, inadequate internet connectivity, and high costs for ride-hailing and delivery (Driving Southeast Asia Forward Through People-Focused Design, n.d.). While Grab promotes digital literacy and financial inclusion for marginalized groups, infrastructure deficiencies in rural areas hinder service expansion and create disparities in availability (Grab Singapore, 2021). Additionally, the digital divide persists, as many rural regions lack the necessary infrastructure and skills to effectively utilize Grab’s platform. Although initiatives for digital literacy training are underway, they remain in development (Montangero, Regmi, & Slaria, 2021). For low-income individuals, service costs remain a significant barrier, though Grab is working to improve affordability through programs like GrabElectric, with ongoing efforts to reach rural populations (Grab Singapore, 2024).


Regulatory and Labor Issues

Grab Philippines is facing misconduct allegations related to driver treatment, particularly after announcing fare reductions—dropping the minimum base fare for food deliveries from ₱45 to ₱35 and the per-kilometer rate from ₱10 to ₱7 (ANNA FELICIA BAJO, GMA Integrated News, 2023). These changes sparked protests among drivers, some of whom were subsequently banned or terminated by the company. Despite Grab's emphasis on benefits for its partners, concerns regarding income stability and fair treatment persist among drivers. In contrast, research from the University of Asia and the Pacific (UA&P) highlights Grab's significant contribution to the national economy, estimating that every peso spent through the app adds ₱3.42 to the GDP (UA&P, 2024). The pandemic has increased reliance on Grab, boosting its net worth and the economy. Moreover, an independent Asian Development Bank (ADB) study noted that Grab provides flexible job opportunities, helping to address unemployment and attracting workers with its flexible work environment and stable income (Grab Philippines, 2023).


Environmental Impact

Grab is dedicated to reducing carbon emissions and promoting a greener environment while serving its partners. The company's 2023 ESG report shows an increase in carbon emissions due to heightened demand for transportation as the world normalizes, though emissions per kilometer for order fulfillment have decreased. In June 2024, Grab Philippines partnered with electric vehicle manufacturer BYD to introduce electric vehicles for its riders, further lowering carbon emissions (Grab Philippines, 2024). However, traffic congestion remains a significant issue in the Philippines, with Grab contributing to the problem. This has led to mixed feelings among residents about whether to protest against Grab's impact on traffic or to continue relying on its convenient services, despite concerns over high fares and increased congestion.
